Class Q Permitted Development

I Converted Three Agricultural Barns. Here Is What I Learned.

Three Class Q applications across two Warwickshire locations. No planning consultant. All granted. The process is not what most people expect.

The first application taught me what the legislation actually says, as opposed to what most people in the industry believe it says. Those two things are not always the same.

The second taught me that highways will be your biggest problem, and that the time to find that out is before you submit, not after the consultation response arrives.

The third confirmed that an open hay barn on a steel frame can be converted under Class Q. Multiple planning professionals told me it could not be done. They were wrong. The case law that changed everything in 2016 had not reached them.

What nobody tells you before you start

The process looks straightforward from the outside. Submit documents, wait 56 days, get a decision. In practice there are a dozen points at which an application can stall, fail, or succeed on a technicality that has nothing to do with the quality of the building or the merits of the proposal.

Most of those points are entirely manageable if you know they are coming. Almost none of them are covered in the general guidance available to farmers.

Why I wrote the guide

After the third application I had a body of knowledge that did not exist anywhere in one place. Not in a form that a farmer, with no planning background and no desire to pay a consultant thousands of pounds, could actually use.

The Class Q Farmers Guide is that knowledge. Every section covers something I encountered directly. Every warning is based on something that nearly went wrong, or did go wrong and had to be recovered.
