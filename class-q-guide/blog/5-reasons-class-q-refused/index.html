The 5 Things That Will Get Your Class Q Application Refused

Tamzin Hennessy  ·  May 2026

Class Q prior approval is not a rubber stamp. Councils refuse applications every week, usually for the same reasons. If you know what they are before you submit, you are already ahead of most applicants.

I have been through the Class Q process on a 90-acre farm in Warwickshire. Two barns. Two applications. Planning committee hearings, highways battles, structural surveys. I have seen what gets refused and why.

Here are the five things that trip people up most often.

The Five Reasons

The building does not qualify, and nobody checked

Not every agricultural building is eligible for Class Q. The rules around when it was built, how it was used, and what it was used for are specific. Many applicants assume eligibility without confirming it. Councils spot this immediately. If the building falls outside the qualifying criteria, the application fails at the first hurdle, no matter how strong everything else is.

The structural argument is not made properly

Class Q is permitted development for conversion, not construction. That distinction matters. The council has to be satisfied that what you are proposing is a conversion of the existing structure, not a rebuild. If your design removes too much of the original fabric, or if the structural survey does not make the case clearly, the council will refuse on the grounds that it amounts to a new dwelling. This is one of the most common refusal reasons and one of the most avoidable.

Highways gets an objection in

Access is scrutinised on almost every rural application. The question is not just whether there is a road. It is whether that road is suitable for residential use, whether visibility splays meet standards, and whether the highways authority is satisfied with the proposed access arrangements. A highways objection can kill an application that is strong on every other ground. The key is anticipating it and addressing it in the submission, not waiting for the objection to arrive.

The design exceeds what Class Q allows

Class Q limits what you can do to the external appearance of the building. There are restrictions on the extent of new openings, the type of materials, and what constitutes permitted operations. Applications that push beyond those limits give councils grounds to refuse. Ambition is fine. Knowing where the line is drawn is essential.

The location is deemed unsuitable for residential use

Some councils apply a sustainability test to Class Q applications. They look at whether the location is appropriate for a dwelling. Isolated rural buildings with no services and poor access to amenities can face refusal on sustainability grounds. This is not universal, and there are ways to address it. But if you are applying for a remote building, you need to know this argument exists and how to counter it.

None of these are insurmountable. But all of them are easier to handle before you submit than after a refusal lands.

A refused application creates a paper trail. It gives the council a position to defend. It costs you time and sometimes the opportunity altogether.
