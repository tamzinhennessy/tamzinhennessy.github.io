Why Highways Objections Kill More Class Q Applications Than Anything Else

Most applicants spend weeks worrying about the building. The highways officer can stop the whole thing in a single response, and the building has nothing to do with it.

I have been through three Class Q applications across two locations in Warwickshire. All three involved highways. All three were granted.

Highways is the one issue I never see mentioned in the general guidance. It is also the one that catches people out most often. By the time the objection arrives, the surveys are done, the drawings are paid for, and the application is already submitted.

It is not about the building

The highways consultation has nothing to do with the structure you want to convert. It is entirely about access. How vehicles reach the site, and whether that access meets current standards.

On a working farm, access was designed for agricultural vehicles and agricultural purposes. The standards for a residential dwelling are different. That gap is where applications fail.

The problems are not always obvious

Some access issues are visible. Most are not, until someone measures them against the relevant standard for your road type and speed limit. By then, you are either in a negotiation you did not plan for, or facing a refusal you cannot easily appeal.

I have dealt with highways objections on multiple applications. There are ways to address them. There are also situations where no amount of negotiation changes the outcome. Knowing which you are facing, before you start, is what the guide covers.
