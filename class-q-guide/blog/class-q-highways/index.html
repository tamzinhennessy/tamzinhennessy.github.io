Why Highways Objections Kill More Class Q Applications Than Anything Else

Most applicants spend weeks worrying about the building. The highways officer can stop the whole thing in a single response — and the building has nothing to do with it.

I have been through two Class Q applications on a working farm in Warwickshire. Both involved highways battles. Both were won. I am not a planning consultant. I am a farmer who learned, the hard way, that highways is the issue nobody prepares you for.

The planning officer assessing your application will consult the local highways authority. That consultation is standard. What happens next is not always predictable.

What the highways officer is actually looking at

The officer is not assessing your building. They are assessing how vehicles will access it. That means the access track, the junction onto the public highway, visibility splays, and the condition of the road itself.

If any of those fail to meet current standards, you will receive an objection. The objection does not have to be proportionate to the scale of your project. A single dwelling on a quiet farm track can receive the same scrutiny as a housing estate.

The visibility splay problem

This is where most applications run into trouble. Visibility splays are the clear lines of sight required at the point where your access meets the public road. The measurements required depend on the speed of the road. On a rural road with a 60mph limit, those measurements are significant.

If there is a hedge, a wall, a bank, or a bend that reduces visibility below the required distance, you have a problem. The highways officer will object. The LPA cannot approve the application over a highways objection without very good reason.

What you can do about it

You can address a highways objection. It is not always fatal. But you need to know what you are dealing with before you submit, not after the objection lands.

That means walking your access. Measuring it. Photographing it. Knowing what the standard is for your road type before the highways officer tells you.

In some cases, a Section 184 agreement — a formal agreement to carry out works to the highway — resolves the issue. In others, the visibility can be improved by cutting back vegetation or land that you own. In others, it cannot be resolved at all and the application will fail.

Knowing which situation you are in before you spend money on surveys and drawings is the difference between a successful application and an expensive lesson.
